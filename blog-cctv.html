How to Plan a CCTV Installation: What Every Business Owner Needs to Know

CM
Musamali Christopher
IT Technician & Software Developer, Kampala

The most common CCTV mistake I see is this: a business owner buys cameras, mounts them, and then discovers that half the angles are wrong, the footage only stores three days before overwriting, and there is no way to view it remotely. A surveillance system is only as good as the planning behind it — and good planning happens before a single cable is run.

This guide walks you through every decision you need to make before, during, and after a CCTV installation.

Step 1: Define What You Are Protecting Against

Different threats require different camera setups. Are you trying to deter theft at a retail till? Document who enters and leaves the building? Monitor a warehouse at night? Cover a car park? Each of these scenarios calls for different camera types, placements, and resolutions. Be specific about your goals before you select any equipment.

Step 2: Plan Camera Placement Before You Buy

Walk through your premises and identify your key coverage zones: entry and exit points, cash handling areas, storage rooms, car parks, and any blind spots where incidents have occurred before. Sketch a simple floor plan and mark your proposed camera positions.

For each position, consider: what angle covers the most ground? Where is the nearest power source? Will this camera be exposed to direct sunlight (which causes glare and reduces image quality)? Is there a ceiling or wall within reach for mounting? Answering these questions on paper is far easier than rerouting cable after installation.

Step 3: Choose the Right Camera Types

The main types you will encounter are: bullet cameras (long-range, directional, good for car parks and perimeters), dome cameras (wide-angle, discreet, harder to tamper with, ideal for indoor use), and PTZ cameras (pan-tilt-zoom, controllable, expensive, suited to large open areas). For most small to medium businesses, a combination of dome cameras indoors and bullet cameras at entry points covers the majority of needs.

Resolution matters. At minimum, use 2MP (1080p) cameras for general coverage. For areas where you need to identify faces or read number plates, use 4MP or 8MP cameras for those specific positions.

Step 4: Calculate Storage Requirements

This is where most self-installed systems fall short. How many days of footage do you need to retain? For most businesses, 30 days is the standard minimum — long enough to discover an incident that was not noticed immediately. For higher-security environments, 60 or 90 days is better.

Storage is calculated based on the number of cameras, resolution, frame rate, and compression format. A rough rule: a single 1080p camera recording continuously at standard compression uses approximately 10–15GB per day. For 8 cameras over 30 days, that is around 2.4–3.6TB. Buy a DVR/NVR with more storage capacity than you currently need — your camera count will likely grow.

Step 5: Plan Your Cable Routes

Poor cable management is the mark of an unprofessional installation and causes real problems — cables get damaged, create tripping hazards, look unsightly, and are a security risk (someone can simply cut an exposed cable). Plan routes that keep cables hidden: inside walls where possible, in conduit or cable trunking along ceilings and skirting boards, and never across open floor areas.

For IP cameras, use Cat6 cable — it carries both data and power (PoE) in a single run, which significantly simplifies the installation. For analogue systems, coaxial cable carries video and a separate cable carries power.

Step 6: Configure Remote Viewing

One of the most valuable features of a modern CCTV system is the ability to view live and recorded footage from your phone, from anywhere. To set this up: connect your DVR/NVR to your router, configure port forwarding, and install the manufacturer's app on your phone. Most modern systems also support P2P cloud access which is simpler to configure.

Test remote viewing thoroughly before handing the system over to the client. Check that footage is accessible over both WiFi and mobile data.

Step 7: Train the System Users

A system that no one knows how to use effectively is not a security asset. Ensure the business owner and relevant staff know how to: view live footage, review and export recorded footage for incidents, check that recording is working, and perform basic troubleshooting (what to do if a camera goes offline, how to restart the DVR).
