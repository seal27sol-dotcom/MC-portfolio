5 IT Mistakes Small Businesses in Uganda Are Making (And How to Fix Them)

CM
Musamali Christopher
IT Technician & Software Developer, Kampala

When I walk into a small business in Kampala for the first time, I can usually spot the problems before anyone says a word. A tangle of cables behind the front desk. A desktop running Windows 7. A staff member saying "we just email things to each other" as a file management strategy. These are not small problems — they are ticking clocks.

Here are the five mistakes I see most often, and exactly what to do about each one.

1. No Data Backup Strategy

This is the most dangerous mistake on the list. I have spoken to business owners who lost years of financial records, customer databases, and transaction histories because a hard drive failed — or a laptop was stolen. In Uganda, power surges and theft are real and common risks that make this even more likely.

The fix is the 3-2-1 rule: keep three copies of your data, on two different types of storage, with one copy offsite. In practice, this means: your working files on your computer, a daily backup to an external hard drive, and a weekly sync to cloud storage (Google Drive or OneDrive both have affordable plans). This setup costs very little and can save your entire business.

2. Everyone Sharing One Password

I encounter this constantly — one WiFi password, one computer login, one email account that five people use. This is a security problem and a management problem at the same time. When something goes wrong, you cannot trace it. When someone leaves the business, you cannot revoke their access cleanly.

The fix: create individual accounts for every staff member and use Windows Local Users or Google Workspace to manage access. When someone joins, create an account. When someone leaves, disable it. It takes twenty minutes to set up and saves enormous headaches.

3. Ignoring Software Updates

The "remind me later" button on Windows Update is the most clicked button in small business IT. I understand why — updates can seem disruptive. But outdated software is one of the most common entry points for malware and ransomware attacks, and these attacks are increasingly targeting small businesses because they are easier targets than large companies.

The fix: schedule updates for off-hours (Saturday night, for example) using Windows Update settings. For critical systems, set updates to install automatically. This is a five-minute change that dramatically reduces your risk.

4. No Antivirus on Business Machines

Windows Defender — the built-in antivirus that comes with Windows 10 and 11 — is genuinely good and completely free. But I still find business computers with it disabled, or machines still running Windows 7 that cannot receive security patches at all. Using a USB drive between computers without scanning it first is standard practice in many offices I visit.

The fix: ensure Windows Defender is active on every machine. Run a full scan weekly. Never plug in an external drive without scanning it first. For higher-risk environments, a paid solution like ESET or Kaspersky is worth the investment.

5. No IT Support Plan

Most small businesses in Kampala call an IT person when something breaks. This reactive approach is always more expensive and more disruptive than a proactive one. A server that goes down on a Monday morning during business hours costs far more in lost productivity than a scheduled maintenance visit on a Sunday would have.

The fix: agree a basic monthly or quarterly maintenance arrangement with a trusted IT technician. Even two hours a month of proactive checks — updates, backup verification, hardware inspection — can prevent the kind of failures that shut businesses down for days.

Final Thought

None of these fixes require a large IT budget. Most require only time and discipline. The businesses that invest in getting these basics right are the ones that do not end up losing data, getting hacked, or going dark for days when a computer fails. Start with one item from this list this week.
