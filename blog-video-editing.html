Getting Started with Video Editing Using Filmora

CM
Musamali Christopher
IT Technician, Software Developer & Designer, Kampala

Video has become the dominant format for marketing, training, events, and communication. Businesses that can produce clean, professional video content in-house have a significant advantage over those that either skip video entirely or outsource every piece of content. Filmora is the tool I recommend for most people starting out — it strikes the right balance between power and accessibility.

This guide covers the core concepts and techniques you need to understand to get productive in Filmora quickly. It is not a click-by-click tutorial — it is a map of what matters.

Understanding the Timeline

Everything in video editing happens on the timeline. The timeline is a horizontal representation of your video over time — clips on the left are earlier, clips on the right are later. In Filmora, video clips sit on video tracks, audio on audio tracks, and text, graphics, and effects on overlay tracks above the main video. Understanding this layering system is the single most important concept in video editing. A lower track is underneath a higher track — so a text overlay on track V2 sits visually on top of your video footage on track V1.

Before you do anything else, learn to navigate the timeline confidently: how to zoom in for precision editing on short sections, how to zoom out to see the full project, and how to move clips by dragging them.

The Cuts That Matter Most

Most beginner editors keep too much footage. Professional editing is primarily an act of removal — cutting everything that does not serve the story or the message. In Filmora, learn the Split tool (usually mapped to the S key or Ctrl+B): position the playhead where you want to cut, split, then delete the section you do not need. This alone handles 80% of editing work.

The other cut to master early is the J-cut and L-cut — where audio and video transitions do not happen at the same point. For example, you might hear someone's voice before you cut to their face, or keep their voice running over a B-roll shot after you have cut away. These techniques make edits feel smooth rather than abrupt.

Colour Correction vs Colour Grading

These are two separate steps that beginners often skip entirely. Colour correction is about fixing what is wrong — if your footage is too dark, too bright, too warm, or too cool, colour correction fixes it so that the image looks accurate and consistent across all your clips. Filmora includes a basic colour correction panel (exposure, contrast, highlights, shadows, white balance) that handles this well.

Colour grading is a creative choice made after correction — applying a look or mood to your footage. A warm grade feels intimate and personal. A cool, desaturated grade feels corporate and serious. A high-contrast cinematic look feels dramatic. In Filmora, LUTs (Look Up Tables) apply grades in one click. Start with correction first, grade second, and always do both on an adjustment layer above your footage so you can adjust or remove it without touching the original clips.

Audio Is Half the Quality

Poor audio destroys otherwise good video. Your audience will tolerate average picture quality but will abandon bad audio within seconds. In Filmora, apply these to every project: use the noise reduction tool to remove background hiss, fan noise, or ambient sound from recorded speech. Normalise your audio levels so spoken content sits consistently around -12 to -6dB. Add a subtle fade-in and fade-out to every audio clip to eliminate hard pops and clicks at cut points. If your original audio is genuinely unusable, record a voiceover in a quiet environment and replace it entirely.

Text, Titles, and Lower Thirds

Filmora has a solid built-in title library. For professional-looking content, keep text simple: one clear font, high contrast against the background, and consistent positioning. Lower thirds (the text that appears in the lower third of the frame to identify a speaker or location) should be brief — name and role or name and location, nothing more. Avoid animated titles that are so elaborate they distract from the content. The title should support the video, not compete with it.

Exporting Correctly

The wrong export settings waste hours of upload time and produce files that look worse than the original. For most use cases: export at 1080p (1920×1080), H.264 codec, frame rate matching your footage (usually 25fps or 30fps), and a bitrate of 8–12 Mbps for good quality at a manageable file size. For social media specifically — Facebook, Instagram, YouTube — these settings produce files that upload quickly and play without quality loss. For archival purposes or professional delivery, use a higher bitrate or export as ProRes if the client requires it.

Building a Simple Workflow

Before you open Filmora for a new project, organise your assets: create a project folder, sort raw footage into subfolders by day or scene, and name your files meaningfully. Filmora's project files reference the original footage — if you move files after importing them, the project loses track of them. A few minutes of organisation before you start saves hours of frustration later.

My standard workflow: import and organise, build a rough cut on the timeline (put everything in order, cut obviously bad takes), refine the edit (tighten timing, cut unnecessary pauses), colour correct and grade, mix audio, add titles and graphics, export. Following a consistent sequence prevents you from jumping between steps and missing something.

Ready to Start?

The best way to learn video editing is to edit a real project — not a tutorial project, but something that actually matters to you or a client. Set a specific deadline, commit to finishing it, and learn what you need to as you go. Filmora's interface is genuinely approachable, and the fundamentals I have covered here will carry you through the vast majority of projects you will encounter.
